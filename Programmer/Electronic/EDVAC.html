Biography

John von Neumann, (born December 28, 1903, Budapest, Hungary—died February 8, 1957, Washington, D.C., U.S.), Hungarian-born American mathematician. As an adult, he appended von to his surname; the hereditary title had been granted his father in 1913. Von Neumann grew from child prodigy to one of the world’s foremost mathematicians by his mid-twenties. Important work in set theory inaugurated a career that touched nearly every major branch of mathematics. Von Neumann’s gift for applied mathematics took his work in directions that influenced quantum theory, automata theory, economics, and defense planning. Von Neumann pioneered game theory and, along with Alan Turing and Claude Shannon, was one of the conceptual inventors of the stored-program digital computer.

Early Life
Von Neumann grew up in an affluent, highly assimilated Jewish family. His father, Miksa Neumann (Max Neumann), was a banker, and his mother, born Margit Kann (Margaret Kann), came from a family that had prospered selling farm equipment. Von Neumann showed signs of genius in early childhood: he could joke in Classical Greek and, for a family stunt, he could quickly memorize a page from a telephone book and recite its numbers and addresses.
Electronic Discrete Variable Automatic Computer

Constructed as a successor to the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC), the Electronic Discrete Variable Automatic Computer (EDVAC) emerged in the early 1950s under the guidance of visionaries John W. Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert, presenting a pivotal shift in computing history by introducing the stored-program concept. This innovative architectural approach, where both data and instructions were stored within the computer's memory, marked a transformative milestone, shaping the trajectory of modern computing and setting the stage for subsequent advancements in electronic data processing. EDVAC's legacy endures through its influential role in the evolution of computer design and its contribution to the fundamental principles that underpin contemporary computing systems.

The development of EDVAC is associated with John von Neumann, who played a significant role in its design. Programming for EDVAC was carried out by various individuals, including Herman Goldstine and John Mauchly.
